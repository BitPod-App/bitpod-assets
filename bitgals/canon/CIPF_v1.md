# Cinematic Identity Persistence Framework v1

**Path:** `bitgals/canon/CIPF_v1.md`  
**Scope:** BitGals canon only, for now.  
**Status:** v1 working doctrine. Expected to evolve after testing across multiple personas.  
**Primary proven test case:** Vera ₿, cyan-charcoal era.

## 1. Purpose

CIPF exists to prevent BitGals from collapsing into generic AI-generated character art.

The goal is not to generate “a themed woman.”

The goal is to preserve a persistent cinematic identity across changing poses, lighting, wardrobe, environments, emotional states, and narrative contexts.

A successful BitGal image should feel as if the character truly existed in the photographed frame, not as if a costume, color palette, or logo was painted over a generic person.

Core invariant:

> Preserve human identity continuity first. Archetype transformation is subordinate to identity persistence.

## 2. The Scaffold Principle

The reference human is not disposable scaffolding.

The scaffold is the reality-anchor through which the archetype manifests.

A strong source photograph already solves many hard problems:

- posture authenticity
- facial asymmetry
- lens behavior
- environmental grounding
- body weight distribution
- imperfect realism
- unconscious expression
- plausible scene physics

CIPF treats the source image as a reality-pressure system. The BitGal archetype must negotiate with that reality instead of replacing it.

The correct operation is:

> preserve the human substrate, then apply the BitGal identity skin.

The incorrect operation is:

> replace the person with a generic archetype-average beauty image.

## 3. Canonical Hierarchy

CIPF uses a strict hierarchy. Lower tiers may change freely only when higher tiers remain intact.

### Tier 1 — Identity Invariants

These define personhood continuity. They should never be casually altered.

Examples:

- orbital spacing
- jaw geometry
- nose structure
- cheek volume
- mouth geometry
- mouth asymmetry
- eye tension
- gaze cognition
- resting emotional state
- posture habits
- silhouette tendencies
- implied cognitive tempo

If Tier 1 drifts, the character dies.

### Tier 2 — Archetypal Traits

These express the BitGal’s canonical role and visual language.

Examples for Vera ₿:

- cyan-charcoal identity language
- disciplined analytical restraint
- dark charcoal base with cyan integration
- asymmetrical cyan bob or cyan-accented structured hair
- clear or technical glasses where appropriate
- verification energy
- technical minimalism
- emotionally compressed intelligence

Examples for Ember ₿:

- wildfire / cultural ignition
- kinetic asymmetry
- ember-orange / copper-fire hair language
- emotionally alive motion
- nightlife / backstage / public-cultural energy
- conviction without permission-seeking

Tier 2 traits may evolve, but only through canon review.

### Tier 3 — Cinematic Variables

These should vary aggressively during storytelling.

Examples:

- pose
- lens
- focal length
- lighting
- weather
- wardrobe
- age
- fatigue
- injury
- emotional state
- setting
- scene genre
- crop
- motion blur
- distance from camera
- partial obstruction
- glamour level

If Tier 3 never changes, the character becomes an avatar rather than a cinematic identity.

## 4. Correct Generation Hierarchy

Use this order when transforming a reference image into a BitGal image:

1. Preserve photographic truth.
2. Preserve human identity continuity.
3. Apply the BitGal archetype.
4. Adapt canon traits to the scene constraints.
5. Add environmental storytelling last.

Wrong order:

1. Cyberpunk aesthetic.
2. Color palette.
3. Logo or symbolic branding.
4. Generic attractive woman.
5. Forced scene afterward.

That wrong order produces aesthetic drift and character death.

## 5. Portability Test

A BitGal identity is portable only when the character remains recognizable under transformation.

The question is not:

> Does this look like the canonical portrait?

The question is:

> Would I believe this character existed here, in this frame, under these conditions?

Identity portability means the character can survive:

- daylight
- profile views
- candid crops
- outdoor scenes
- awkward angles
- low-glam realism
- different hairstyles
- non-hero lighting
- emotional ambiguity
- scene-specific wardrobe
- partial obstruction
- non-obvious narrative context

Real people survive these changes. BitGals must as well.

## 6. Diegetic Believability

A successful image should feel diegetic: as if it belongs inside a larger world already in motion.

A strong BitGal image should feel like a paused frame from a film, not a poster made to show off a character design.

Diegetic details may include:

- unexplained tools
- field equipment
- worn materials
- environmental traces
- off-screen implications
- body language suggesting prior action
- tension between character and setting

The best details do not explain themselves immediately. They imply a world.

## 7. Failure Modes

### 7.1 Cosmetic Drift

The character is reduced to surface markers:

- hair color
- outfit
- logo
- palette
- vibe words

Result: generic themed character.

### 7.2 Latent Replacement

The model replaces the source human with an archetype-average beauty template.

Result: every BitGal becomes the same latent-space woman in different costumes.

### 7.3 Aesthetic Dominance

Lighting, styling, or environment overpower identity continuity.

Result: beautiful image, dead character.

### 7.4 Canonical Fragility

The character is recognizable only in controlled conditions:

- frontal hero portrait
- perfect lighting
- direct gaze
- familiar wardrobe
- repeated camera angle

Result: the identity collapses under cinematic pressure.

### 7.5 Logo Contamination

Symbols such as ₿ marks, patches, badges, or text are overused as identity substitutes.

Result: branding screams because character continuity failed.

Rule:

> Branding should support identity, never replace it.

### 7.6 Genre Cliché Capture

The character collapses into the nearest genre stereotype.

Examples:

- generic cyberpunk hacker girl
- gamer-RGB streamer
- neon sci-fi model
- crypto influencer
- rave girl
- AI waifu
- corporate futurist mascot

Result: archetype replaced by cliché.

## 8. Identity Stress Tests

Use difficult reference images deliberately. Weak identity systems only work under flattering, controlled conditions.

Recommended stress tests:

- profile view
- three-quarter rear view
- daylight
- candid crop
- motion blur
- outdoor realism
- harsh shadows
- reflective surfaces
- low-resolution source
- strange props
- emotional exhaustion
- partial obstruction
- wide-angle distortion
- aging or time passage
- wardrobe far outside default canon
- non-obvious scene context

Passing a stress test means the character still feels like herself without relying on obvious canon crutches.

## 9. Persona Notes: Vera ₿ v1 Evidence

Vera’s portability became convincing when the image generation stopped trying to replace the source woman with “cyberpunk QA girl” and instead preserved the source woman’s posture, facial structure, and photographic truth while applying Vera’s cyan-charcoal canon.

What worked:

- preserving the reference woman’s posture and angle
- keeping the scene physically grounded
- using cyan as identity language, not decoration
- avoiding excessive ₿ branding
- maintaining Vera’s analytical restraint
- making the character feel cognitively dangerous rather than merely aesthetically cool
- allowing outdoor concrete realism to remove cyberpunk crutches
- letting an unusual shoulder/metal element become diegetic instead of forcing an explanation

Vera’s image succeeded when it felt like:

> Vera inhabiting the photograph.

Not:

> a photograph replaced by a Vera-themed render.

## 10. Early Scoring Direction

CIPF should eventually evolve into a calculator-scoring mechanism similar in spirit to BitRegime’s reality-scoring problem.

The purpose would be to measure success against canon and photographic reality instead of relying only on subjective taste.

Potential scoring dimensions:

| Dimension | Question | Draft Weight |
|---|---|---:|
| Identity Continuity | Does the person remain recognizably the same underlying character? | 30 |
| Scaffold Preservation | Did the output preserve pose, geometry, camera logic, and scene truth? | 20 |
| Archetype Fidelity | Did the persona’s canonical traits manifest without overpowering the person? | 20 |
| Diegetic Believability | Does the image feel like a frame from a real world/story? | 15 |
| Anti-Cliché Resistance | Did it avoid generic genre collapse? | 10 |
| Branding Discipline | Are symbols/logos restrained and subordinate? | 5 |

Draft total: 100.

A future scoring pass should define:

- numeric ranges
- hard fails
- persona-specific modifiers
- stress-test multipliers
- reviewer notes
- before/after comparison fields
- evidence anchors
- image metadata requirements

Possible future rule:

> A BitGal image is production-candidate only if it scores high on identity continuity and scaffold preservation, even if its aesthetics are imperfect.

## 11. Working Mantra

> Archetypes are portable only when identity is anchored deeply enough to survive reality.
